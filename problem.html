Unchecked Use of Data Extraction Tools

• The problem exists within the infrastructure and systems used by various federal departments and agencies in Canada. Personal data is being extracted using data extraction tools, capable of recovering and analyzing data from electronic devices. These data could include:

  • Text messages
  • Contacts
  • Photos
  • Travel history
  • Internet search history
  • Deleted content
  • Social media activity
• Federal departments have only said they use these tools after they’ve obtained a judicial authorization, such as a search warrant. Some departments have said that these data are used for investigations such as workplace harassment. However, other departments claim they collect personal data “for security reasons”. People are concerned about the transparency and accountability of what data is being accessed and how these departments manage them (CBC News, 2023).

Audits and reports, including those from the Auditor General, reveal significant inconsistencies in how cybersecurity measures are applied across Canadian federal departments. While security protocols exist, their implementation often varies due to differing interpretations of requirements, outdated systems, and a lack of cohesive policies. Some departments fail to adopt robust encryption, real-time monitoring, or consistent compliance practices, leaving systems vulnerable to exploitation. Additionally, legacy software and infrastructure exacerbate these risks, as they are often ill-equipped to handle modern cyber threats (Government of Canada, 2020).

These inconsistencies create critical vulnerabilities, allowing malicious actors to exploit weak links in the government’s defense. This fragmentation increases the likelihood of cyberattacks, leading to the loss of sensitive citizen data, disruption of essential government services, and harm to national security. Addressing this issue requires standardized policies, regular audits, and modernization of IT infrastructure to build a unified and resilient cybersecurity framework capable of protecting Canada’s digital assets (Williams, 2023).

The Canadian government struggles with defining clear roles and responsibilities for cybersecurity, which creates a fragmented approach to managing risks. Many departments do not assign specific accountability for cybersecurity, which hinders effective governance and oversight. Additionally, a lack of long-term funding strategies results in insufficient resources for modernizing infrastructure, adopting secure cloud solutions, and addressing evolving cyber threats.

The absence of consistent leadership and funding limits the government's ability to proactively address cybersecurity challenges. Without clear accountability, cyber incidents are not managed efficiently, and departments may fail to detect vulnerabilities in time. Moreover, the lack of a comprehensive, sustainable funding approach means cybersecurity initiatives are often reactive, which increases the risk of data breaches and compromises citizens' privacy (Office of the Auditor General of Canada, 2022).
